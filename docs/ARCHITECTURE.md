# Architecture

The engine is a layered digital twin. Each layer is a small, defensible module in
`qcom/`, and the layers compose into the unit economics of one dark store.

## Data flow

```
 GridCity (qcom/geo.py)
   density grid + road distances
        |
        v
 DemandModel (qcom/demand.py)
   gravity/Huff catchment: density grid + store cells -> orders/day per store
        |
        v
 DarkStoreSim (qcom/desim.py)
   discrete-event store: arrivals -> picking queue -> rider dispatch + batching
   outputs: delivery-time distribution, SLA breach, rider/picker utilisation,
            realised per-order rider + picking cost
        |
        v
 CostModel (qcom/costs.py)            calibrate.py
   per-order P&L  <--------------- solves fixed cost so the metro regime
   contribution = revenue           reproduces the published loss-per-order
     - packaging - rider
     - picking - fixed
        |
        +--> FacilityLocator (qcom/place.py)   greedy + local search + LP bound
        +--> frontier (qcom/frontier.py)       Nelder-Mead breakeven search
        +--> sensitivity (qcom/sensitivity.py) Saltelli + Jansen Sobol indices
        |
        v
 scenarios.py (the twin glue) -> run_experiments.py / cli.py / app/wargame.py
```

## The layers

### GridCity (geo.py)
A square demand grid. Each cell has a population density and a (lat, lon). Travel
is along straight-line distance inflated by a detour factor (a stand-in for the
street network). `synthetic()` builds a monocentric/polycentric density surface so
the dense-core / sparse-edge structure that drives the economics is present.

### DemandModel (demand.py)
Huff's gravity model. Each demand cell's orders are allocated across in-range stores
in proportion to attractiveness over distance raised to a decay exponent. Demand
beyond the service radius leaks out of all catchments, which is exactly the
coverage gap the facility-location optimizer tries to close.

### DarkStoreSim (desim.py)
The defensible core. A binary-heap (`heapq`) event loop processes four event types:
order arrival, pick completion, rider return, and a one-shot batching flush.

- Arrivals are a non-homogeneous Poisson process with morning and evening peaks,
  generated by thinning a homogeneous process at the peak rate.
- Picking is a finite roster of pickers serving a FIFO queue with lognormal service
  times (an M/M/c-style station).
- Rider dispatch batches up to `batch_target` orders per trip, routes them
  nearest-neighbour over the road graph, and adds the return leg. A one-shot flush
  event per staged order guarantees no order waits past its batching window, which
  bounds the event count and avoids the self-rescheduling loop an earlier design
  hit.

Rider and picker costs are roster costs (provisioned for the peak, paid whole days)
amortised over delivered orders, so low density shows up as idle capacity and a high
per-order cost. That is the mechanism behind the tier-2 hole.

### CostModel and calibration (costs.py, calibrate.py)
Per-order contribution = merchandise margin + ad revenue - packaging - rider -
picking - fixed. The merchandise take is a thin net margin (grocery is low margin).
The simulation injects its realised rider and picking cost. Calibration solves for
the one hard-to-observe parameter, the dark-store fixed cost per day, so the metro
twin reproduces the published metro loss-per-order. Only then is the tier-2
extrapolation trusted.

### FacilityLocator (place.py)
Maximal-covering facility location. Greedy seeding by marginal demand gain, then
1-swap local search. An LP relaxation (via `scipy.optimize.linprog`, HiGHS) gives an
upper bound on coverage, so the heuristic's optimality gap is reported, not assumed.

### frontier (frontier.py)
The simulator is an expensive, noisy black box. A from-scratch Nelder-Mead simplex
minimises a weighted "effort" to move levers up from the tier-2 baseline, with a
penalty for failing the contribution target or the SLA cap. `frontier_curve`
bisects the ad take needed at each density to trace the breakeven frontier, and
`aov_only_strawman` shows the premium-basket playbook failing on density.

### sensitivity (sensitivity.py)
Sobol global sensitivity from scratch: Saltelli sampling builds the A, B and A_B^i
matrices, and the Jansen estimators give first-order and total indices. This is a
variance-based attribution across the whole input space, not a one-at-a-time
tornado, so the "density and batching, not basket" claim is measured.

## Why this shape

- **Simulation, not a formula**, because the deciding quantity is counterfactual
  (the margin of a configuration that does not yet exist) and is driven by queueing
  and routing that a static P&L cannot capture.
- **Roster costs amortised over volume**, because that is the real fixed-cost-over-
  low-density mechanism that creates the tier-2 hole.
- **Calibrate before extrapolating**, so the model earns trust on the regime where
  a real number exists before predicting the regime where one does not.
- **Bounds and attribution everywhere** (LP bound on placement, Sobol on levers), so
  every claim is auditable rather than asserted.
